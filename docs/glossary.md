# Glossary: Technical Terminology

## A

**Action Space**: The set of all possible actions that an agent can take in an environment.

**Actuator**: A mechanical device for moving or controlling a mechanism or system.

**Affordance**: The possibility of an action on an object or environment.

**AI (Artificial Intelligence)**: The simulation of human intelligence processes by machines, especially computer systems.

**Autonomous System**: A system that can operate independently without human intervention.

## B

**Behavior Tree**: A mathematical model of plan execution used in robotics and AI.

**Biomechanics**: The study of the structure, function, and motion of the mechanical aspects of biological systems.

**Body Schema**: A representation of the body's configuration in space.

## C

**Cognitive Architecture**: An overarching structure of the mind that determines how information is processed.

**Computer Vision**: A field that trains computers to interpret and understand the visual world.

**Control Theory**: An engineering and mathematics branch that deals with the behavior of dynamical systems.

**Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)**: A class of deep neural networks used for image analysis.

**Cognitive Robotics**: A branch of robotics that focuses on creating robots with cognitive capabilities.

## D

**Deep Learning**: A subset of machine learning based on artificial neural networks.

**Digital Twin**: A virtual representation of a physical object or system.

**Dynamical Systems**: Mathematical formalizations for systems with state that evolves over time.

**Dexterity**: The skill and elegance in using one's hands.

## E

**Embodied AI**: Artificial intelligence that is designed to interact with the physical world.

**Embodiment**: The physical form and its interaction with the environment as a basis for intelligence.

**End-Effector**: The device at the end of a robotic arm designed to interact with the environment.

**Episodic Memory**: A collection of past personal experiences that occurred at a particular time and place.

## F

**Forward Kinematics**: The use of joint parameters to compute the configuration of the kinematic chain.

**Fuzzy Logic**: A form of many-valued logic in which the truth values are truth degrees.

## G

**Gaussian Process**: A stochastic process, a collection of random variables with a joint Gaussian distribution.

**Gazebo**: A 3D simulation environment for robotics.

**Generalization**: The ability of a model to perform well on unseen data.

**Gripper**: A device that grasps an object.

## H

**Human-Robot Interaction (HRI)**: The field of study surrounding the design, development, and evaluation of robots for human interaction.

**Haptic Feedback**: The use of touch in human-computer interaction.

**Heuristic**: A technique designed for solving a problem more quickly when classic methods are too slow.

**Holonomic**: A system where the number of controllable degrees of freedom equals total degrees of freedom.

## I

**Inverse Kinematics**: The mathematical process of calculating the variable joint parameters needed to place the end of a kinematic chain.

**Imitation Learning**: Learning by observing and replicating human behavior.

**Intentionality**: The power of minds to be about, represent, or stand for things, properties and states of affairs.

## J

**Joint Space**: The space defined by the joint angles of a robot.

**Jacobian Matrix**: A matrix of partial derivatives used in robotics to relate joint velocities to end-effector velocities.

## K

**Kinematics**: The study of motion without considering the forces that cause the motion.

**Kinesthetic**: Relating to the sensation of movement.

**Knowledge Representation**: An area of artificial intelligence focused on how knowledge is stored and used by AI systems.

## L

**Latent Space**: A space whose dimensions represent underlying factors of variation in data.

**Learning Rate**: A hyperparameter that controls how much to change the model in response to the estimated error each time the model weights are updated.

**Lidar**: A remote sensing method that uses light in the form of a pulsed laser to measure distances.

**Locomotion**: The ability to move from place to place.

## M

**Manipulation**: The skillful handling of objects.

**Markov Decision Process (MDP)**: A mathematical framework for modeling decision-making in situations where outcomes are partly random and partly under the control of a decision maker.

**Motion Planning**: The computational problem of finding a sequence of valid configurations to move an object from a source to a destination.

**Machine Learning**: The study of computer algorithms that can improve automatically through experience.

**Morphology**: The study of the form and structure of organisms.

## N

**Natural Language Processing (NLP)**: A branch of AI that helps computers understand, interpret and manipulate human language.

**Neural Network**: A series of algorithms that endeavors to recognize underlying relationships in a set of data through a process that mimics the way the human brain operates.

**Non-holonomic**: A system with constraints that cannot be integrated to form a set of position constraints.

## O

**Ontology**: A formal naming and definition of the categories, properties, and relations between the concepts that exist in a domain.

**Open Source**: Software with source code that anyone can inspect, modify, and enhance.

**Operational Space**: The space defined by the position and orientation of the end-effector.

## P

**Path Planning**: The process of determining how to move from one location to another.

**Perception**: The process of becoming aware of something through the senses.

**PID Controller**: A control loop mechanism employing feedback in industrial control systems.

**Probabilistic Robotics**: A field that deals with the uncertainty inherent in robot sensing and control.

**Point Cloud**: A collection of data points in a coordinate system.

## Q

**Quaternion**: A mathematical concept used to represent rotations in 3D space.

## R

**Reinforcement Learning**: A type of machine learning algorithm that allows an agent to learn in an environment by trial and error.

**ROS (Robot Operating System)**: A flexible framework for writing robot software.

**ROS 2**: The second generation of the Robot Operating System.

**Robotics**: The branch of technology that deals with the design, construction, operation, and application of robots.

**Robotics Middleware**: Software that provides common services and capabilities to applications outside what's offered by the operating system.

**Reactive System**: A system that responds to changes in its environment.

## S

**Sensor Fusion**: The combining of sensory data or data derived from disparate sources.

**SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping)**: The computational problem of constructing or updating a map of an unknown environment while simultaneously keeping track of an agent's location within it.

**State Space**: The set of all possible states of a system.

**Supervised Learning**: The machine learning task of learning a function that maps an input to an output based on example input-output pairs.

**Stereopsis**: The perception of depth and 3D structure obtained on the basis of visual input from two eyes.

## T

**Task Planning**: The process of determining a sequence of actions to achieve a goal.

**Teleoperation**: The remote control of a machine by a human operator.

**Trajectory**: The path that a moving object follows through space as a function of time.

**Transformer**: A deep learning model that uses attention mechanisms to weigh the importance of input data.

## U

**Unsupervised Learning**: A type of algorithm that learns patterns from untagged data.

**Uncanny Valley**: The hypothesis that when human replicas look and act almost, but not perfectly, like real human beings, they cause a response of unease or revulsion.

## V

**Vision-Language-Action (VLA)**: Systems that integrate visual perception, natural language understanding, and physical action.

**Virtual Reality (VR)**: A simulated experience that can be similar to or completely different from the real world.

**Volumetric Capture**: A set of technologies that allow the visual capture of a person or object in three dimensions.

## W

**Whole-Body Control**: A control approach that considers the entire robot as a single system.

**Workspace**: The space in which a robot can operate.

## X, Y, Z

**Zero Moment Point (ZMP)**: A criterion for static and dynamic stability of legged robots.

**Z-axis**: The vertical axis in a 3D coordinate system, typically representing height.

**Yaw**: The rotation of an object around its vertical axis.

**ZMP Control**: A method of controlling bipedal robots based on Zero Moment Point stability criterion.
